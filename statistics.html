What I Learned in Statistics

Introduction

Throughout my academic journey in learning statistics, I have gained an understanding of data analysis, probability theory, and various statistical methods used to interpret and make decisions based on data. Below is an overview of the key topics I have explored.

Core Topics in Statistics

  • Descriptive Statistics: Learning about measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode), measures of dispersion (variance, standard deviation), and how to visualize data with charts and graphs.
  • Probability Theory: Understanding the fundamentals of probability, including probability distributions, conditional probability, and Bayes' theorem.
  • Hypothesis Testing: Performing hypothesis tests, including t-tests, chi-squared tests, and ANOVA, to assess whether observed data supports a given hypothesis.
  • Correlation and Regression: Studying how to analyze relationships between variables using correlation coefficients and performing linear regression analysis to make predictions.
  • Sampling Methods: Learning about random sampling, sampling distributions, and how to estimate population parameters based on sample data.
  • Statistical Software: Using statistical software such as R, Python (with libraries like Pandas, NumPy, and SciPy), and SPSS for data analysis and visualization.

Advanced Topics in Statistics

  • Multivariate Analysis: Exploring techniques like principal component analysis (PCA), factor analysis, and cluster analysis to analyze complex datasets with multiple variables.
  • Time Series Analysis: Studying the methods used to analyze data points collected or recorded at specific time intervals, including trend analysis and forecasting models.
  • Bayesian Statistics: Delving into Bayesian inference and methods, where probability distributions are updated as new data becomes available.
  • Non-Parametric Methods: Understanding statistical methods that do not assume a specific data distribution, including the use of the Wilcoxon test and Kruskal-Wallis test.

Best Practices in Statistics

  • Data Cleaning: Learning to preprocess raw data by removing outliers, handling missing values, and transforming variables for analysis.
  • Statistical Inference: Applying statistical reasoning to make predictions and generalizations about populations based on sample data, while considering confidence intervals and margin of error.
  • Visualization: Effectively communicating statistical findings using clear and informative visualizations such as histograms, box plots, and scatter plots.
  • Ethics in Statistics: Understanding the ethical implications of data collection, analysis, and interpretation, including issues related to bias and transparency.
